Let's be Honest

I started web design professionally in 1998.

A hazy time when we were all webmasters, there was no WordPress, CSS wasn't entirely supported inline, nevermind as an external file, and most sites were a mish-mash of animated gifs and inline table styles.

There is no feasible way I can show all 23 years of experience here. Can you imagine trying to show every site I've designed, or every framework I've built? And what I coded in 2002 certainly wouldn't hold up now; what was revolutionary then, is irrelevant now.

What isn't irrelevant, is how all those years of design and CSS taught me to keep learning. To expand my knowledgebase. To understand what I was working around, whether it be PHP, React, Angular or even ColdFusion back in the day, so I could build a better mousetrap around it.

Back then and for a long time, no school had any cirriculum that tackled this. We all right-clicked websites and figured out how to code this on our own.

It taught me to teach myself. Every day, every week, web dev is a constant cycle of learning, of discarding what no longer works, adapting to what our users need and what devices they use.

That is probably one of the only constants in web development; change. And the need to keep up with it.
